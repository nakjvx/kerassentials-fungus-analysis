## Why Toenail Fungus Is Harder to Beat Than You Think

Here's the thing most people don't realize.

Nail fungus — medically called onychomycosis — doesn't just sit on the surface. It burrows under the nail plate, feeds on keratin, and spreads while you're busy hoping that drugstore cream will magically fix it. According to the American Podiatric Medical Association, onychomycosis affects about 3% to 5% of the US population. That's millions of people walking around with yellow, thickened, brittle nails, many of them having tried and failed multiple treatments.

Oral antifungal pills like terbinafine work. But they come with liver toxicity risks and require blood tests. Laser treatments? Expensive. Topical drugstore creams? They often can't penetrate deep enough.

This is why an oil-based formula makes sense. Oils seep into the nail plate more effectively than water-based creams. And when those oils also happen to have antifungal punch — tea tree, lavender, undecylenic acid — you've got a logical approach.

## How to Use Kerassentials: Step-by-Step

Using this stuff isn't complicated. But consistency matters more than anything else. Here's the routine:

1. **Start with clean, completely dry feet.** Wash with soap and water. Dry thoroughly — especially between toes and around nail edges. Fungus loves moisture, so this step isn't skippable.
2. **Use the brush applicator.** Each bottle comes with a built-in brush. Dip it, then coat the entire nail surface — the top, the edges, and under the nail tip if you can reach it.
3. **Apply twice daily.** Once in the morning after your shower, once before bed. Four applications per day are recommended on some product pages for aggressive treatment, but twice daily is the commonly cited baseline.
4. **Massage it in.** Don't just paint it on and walk away. Rub the oil into the nail and surrounding skin for 10-15 seconds to boost absorption and blood flow.
5. **Let it absorb fully.** Wait a minute or two before putting on socks or shoes. You want the oil to sink in, not get wiped off.
6. **Stick with it for at least 4 to 6 weeks.** Toenails grow slowly — about 1-2mm per month. You might see reduced itching and inflammation within the first couple of weeks, but full nail restoration takes time. For long-standing infections, 90 days is a more realistic timeline.

The biggest mistake people make? Stopping too early. When the itching goes away and the nail starts looking pink again, it's tempting to call it done. But the fungus can still be lurking under the regrowth. Finish the full course.
